Mentoroid

How do you help a serious gamer get better when the feedback loop is broken?

Mentoroid is a desktop coaching platform for competitive Dota 2 players — designed to close the gap between playing and actually improving, through real-time guidance and post-game analysis that tells you not just what happened, but what to change.

The Problem

Most Dota 2 players grind for years without actually getting better.

The average competitive Dota 2 player spends hundreds of hours a year in matches — yet most plateau. Not because they lack effort, but because the feedback loop is broken. A match ends. The post-game screen shows K/D/A and gold graphs. Then nothing. No direction. No coaching. No answer to the one question that actually matters: what should I do differently next game?

Dota 2 is one of the most complex games ever made — 120+ heroes, dynamic team roles, three distinct game phases, and hundreds of micro-decisions per match. The skill gap between a stuck player and an improving one isn't mechanical. It's informational. Better players have mental models for reading their own performance. Most players don't — and the game gives them nothing to build one with.

Professional players have coaches. The other 13 million don't. Mentoroid was built to change that — a desktop companion that sits alongside live gameplay, processes real-time match telemetry, and delivers the kind of structured, personalized feedback that until now only existed in paid coaching sessions or top-tier esports organizations.

Why This Is Hard

Building a coaching tool for gamers means solving problems that don't exist in any other product category.

Coaching software for knowledge workers — think Grammarly, Notion AI, or Figma — operates in a calm environment. The user is stationary. The screen is theirs. Time is available. Dota 2 is the opposite: full-screen, time-critical, and cognitively saturated. Every design and engineering decision had to be made with that context in mind.

01

The tool cannot interrupt the game — but it must react to it in real time.

A player in the middle of a match cannot read a dashboard. But coaching delivered five minutes after a mistake is useless. Mentoroid had to operate invisibly during gameplay — consuming minimal CPU and memory — while still capturing live match events and surfacing timely voice cues at the right moments. That's a fundamentally different constraint than any web-based analytics product.

02

Players don't want data. They want to know what to fix.

Dota 2 already exposes match statistics. The problem isn't data availability — it's interpretation. A player staring at a KDA chart doesn't know if their laning phase is the issue, their hero pool is too narrow, or their late-game decision-making is costing wins. Mentoroid needed to synthesize hundreds of data points per match into a single, prioritized answer: here's what's actually holding you back.

03

The product had to work on every serious gamer's machine — without a setup wizard.

Competitive players use macOS, Windows, and Linux. They have Steam installed in non-default directories. They run multiple monitors, custom DPI settings, and sandboxed environments. A coaching tool that requires thirty minutes of configuration to activate loses the player before the first session. The setup experience had to be automatic, invisible, and correct across all environments — or it simply wouldn't be used.

04

AI coaching that fires at the wrong moment destroys trust immediately.

Voice coaching during a game is high-stakes UX. If a coaching cue fires late, fires for the wrong reason, or fails silently after an update, players stop trusting the system — and a tool that isn't trusted isn't used. The entire pipeline from match event detection to voice delivery had to be deterministic, with startup orchestration ensuring every component was registered and ready before the first game began.

Technology Decision

Why Flutter Desktop — and what we gave up to choose it.

The framework decision was made early and shaped everything that followed. Three serious options were on the table: Electron, native platform toolkits (SwiftUI + WinUI + GTK), and Flutter Desktop. Each had real tradeoffs — and the choice wasn't obvious.

❌

Why not Electron?

Electron was the fastest path to a cross-platform desktop app. We ruled it out for one reason: resource consumption. Electron ships a full Chromium instance. On a gaming PC where every CPU and GPU cycle competes with the game, a 300MB+ memory overhead from a companion app is a non-starter. Players would disable it before the first session.

❌

Why not native per-platform?

SwiftUI on macOS, WinUI on Windows, and GTK on Linux would have delivered the best possible performance per platform. But it meant three separate codebases, three UI implementations, and tripled QA surface — for a small team shipping a product that wasn't yet revenue-generating. The maintenance cost would have killed the iteration speed we needed.

✓

Why Flutter Desktop?

Flutter's Skia/Impeller renderer runs GPU-accelerated without a browser engine, keeping the memory footprint low enough to coexist with a running game. A single Dart codebase served all three platforms, with platform-specific logic isolated to native channels. The Dart VM's long-running process behavior — unlike a browser's GC model — made it predictable across 2–3 hour gaming sessions.

⚠️

What we gave up.

Flutter Desktop is not a mature target. Desktop-specific packages are thinner than mobile equivalents. Window management, system tray integration, and native menu bars required workarounds. Some platform behaviors — particularly macOS sandboxing interactions with Steam paths — had no documented solutions and required original investigation. We accepted this tradeoff because the rendering and codebase unification benefits outweighed the rough edges.

The right framework decision isn't the one with the most features — it's the one whose tradeoffs you can live with given your specific constraints. For a small team building a game-adjacent desktop tool across three platforms, Flutter's unification benefits were worth its immaturity.

Technical Approach

Architecture & Design

Mentoroid follows a layered architecture — the codebase is organized by layer first, with all features sharing each layer. State management is handled through Provider with ChangeNotifier, organized into a dependency-chained hierarchy.

Note on structure: Unlike feature-first Clean Architecture where each feature owns its own data/domain/presentation subtree, Mentoroid groups all features together within each layer — e.g. all providers live in presentation/providers/ and all screens under presentation/screens/. The layered separation is maintained; feature-level isolation is not.

Provider Dependency Tree — providers are chained so that downstream providers automatically receive updated data when upstream state changes:

AuthProvider (root — Steam ID, session, tokens)
├── PerformanceProvider (hero stats, win rates, trends)
│   └── GoalSettingProvider (AI goals, coaching, LLM)
├── GameAnalysisProvider (match history, sessions)
│   └── GoalSettingProvider (shared via ProxyProvider)
├── ProfileProvider (user profile, avatar)
├── GsiSetupProvider (installation detection)
└── PGADetailProvider (post-game analysis details)

Engineering Deep Dives

Challenges & Solutions

Challenge — Embedded Backend Lifecycle

Managing a local backend binary within a Flutter Desktop app is non-trivial.

The AI coaching pipeline requires a companion backend binary to run locally alongside the Flutter app. Distributing, securing, and managing this binary's lifecycle is complex.

Solution — Encrypted Binary Distribution

AES-256-CBC encryption with runtime decryption and subprocess management.

The backend binary is compiled for each target platform and encrypted using AES-256-CBC with a 32-byte environment key. The encrypted binary (backend.enc) is bundled in assets, decrypted at runtime to the application support directory, and launched as a subprocess. A webhook server on port 18100 relays match lifecycle events back to the Flutter layer. File integrity is validated by checking for the binary and its _internal/ dependency folder before each launch.

Challenge — Cross-Platform GSI Configuration

Dota 2's GSI config must be placed in a Steam library path that varies wildly by OS.

Steam can be installed in dozens of locations depending on the OS, user, and whether multiple libraries are configured.

Solution — Multi-Path Detection with VDF Parsing

OS-specific detection strategies with Steam library VDF file parsing.

The GSI service implements OS-specific detection: on Windows it queries the registry and falls back to default paths; on macOS it checks both the sandboxed home and real user home (/Users/$USER); on Linux it checks .steam/steam, .local/share/Steam, and .steam/debian-installation. All strategies parse Steam's libraryfolders.vdf file to discover non-default library locations before writing the config file.

Challenge — Real-Time Game Monitoring Without Drain

The app must detect Dota 2 start/stop without a tight polling loop that hurts in-game performance.

Solution — Adaptive Polling with Process Detection

Dynamic interval switching with platform-native process detection commands.

The GameMonitor service uses an adaptive timer strategy: 3-second polling intervals when the game is not running, switching to 10-second intervals once the game process is detected. Platform-specific commands (pgrep -f dota on macOS/Linux, tasklist on Windows) are used for detection. All timers are cancelled cleanly on app exit to prevent resource leaks.

Challenge — Voice Coaching GSI Sync

Early in development, TTS coaching was silent despite being configured.

The GSI config file was not being written, and the player profile was not being registered with the backend before match start — causing a race condition.

Solution — Startup Orchestration with _ensureGsiConfigured()

A dedicated startup orchestration method that resolves the race condition on app launch.

A startup orchestration method runs on app launch ensuring (a) the GSI config file exists and is valid, and (b) set_player is called against the backend before any match begins. This eliminated the race condition between app startup and game launch that was causing silent coaching sessions.

Challenge — Data-Dense UI Without Visual Overload

Dota 2 generates rich telemetry per match. Presenting all of it simultaneously risks overwhelming players.

Solution — Layered Information Architecture

Progressive disclosure through a layered reveal strategy across UI contexts.

The UI uses a layered reveal strategy: the main dashboard shows an aggregated score and top-line metrics; role and hero breakdowns are surfaced in modals on demand; post-game analysis is a separate screen flow. Interactive fl_chart visualizations with hover tooltips give depth to players who want it, without cluttering the primary view for those who don't.

Where We Are

What's been validated — and what's still a hypothesis.

Mentoroid is in active development and has not yet launched publicly. Being honest about that distinction matters: there are things we know, things we've validated internally, and things that remain open questions until real players use it at scale.

All metrics reflect internal pre-launch testing across a team of 3 developers, 2 QA testers, and 4 backend engineers. Public launch metrics will be instrumented via PostHog and Firebase Analytics.

Pipeline validated across 150+ internal sessions.

Across 150+ test sessions on macOS and Windows, the full pipeline — GSI detection, match event capture, AI summary generation, and voice coaching delivery — fired correctly with no silent failures. The startup race condition that caused early silent coaching sessions was eliminated and has not recurred since the orchestration fix.

Zero critical crashes in the last 50 monitored sessions.

Earlier sessions surfaced a crash traced to GCP returning HTTP headers with Latin-encoded bytes — malformed content the Flutter HTTP client did not handle gracefully. The fix was applied at three levels: the backend sanitized headers before transmission, the Flutter layer added defensive error handling to recover rather than crash on bad header content, and the team is upgrading to Flutter 3.41.6 (Dart 3.11.4) which ships an improved HTTP parser that handles these bytes natively — permanently resolving the root cause at the platform level once users update. Sentry has shown zero recurrence across the 50 sessions since.

GSI setup completes in under 60 seconds on all tested environments.

Across macOS (including sandboxed), Windows (default and custom Steam library paths), and Linux, the GSI wizard detects the Dota 2 installation and writes the config file automatically — without any manual path input from the user. Internal testers went from installation to first coaching-ready session in under 60 seconds on every tested configuration.

Whether coaching changes behavior is still an open question.

The core hypothesis — that structured AI coaching translates into measurable rank improvement — has not yet been tested at scale. The PASOLL framework, goal tracking, and session-over-session trend analysis exist specifically to make that measurement possible. Validating it requires real players over a sustained period. That work is ahead of us, and PostHog is instrumented to capture it from day one of public launch.

Key Engineering Decisions Made

Chose Flutter Desktop over Electron specifically to eliminate the resource overhead of a bundled browser engine on gaming hardware

Encrypted backend binary distribution — no plaintext executables in the app bundle, decrypted only at runtime in the application support directory

Adaptive polling strategy (3s → 10s on game detection) keeps the monitoring footprint near zero during active gameplay

Startup orchestration with _ensureGsiConfigured() eliminated the race condition between app launch and game start that was causing silent coaching sessions

Layered information architecture — aggregate score on the dashboard, breakdowns on demand, post-game detail on a separate flow — to avoid overwhelming players who just want one answer

PostHog and Firebase Analytics instrumented from day one to measure feature adoption and session patterns before public launch
